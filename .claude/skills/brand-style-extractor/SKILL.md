## ACTIVATION
Use this skill when: Extracting comprehensive brand guidelines from existing materials, websites, content samples, or brand documents.

**Activate with**: "Extract brand style", "Analyse brand guidelines", "Create brand profile from [materials]"

# PART 1: BRAND AUDIT FRAMEWORK

## What To Collect
Before beginning extraction, gather as many of these as available:

**Tier 1 (Essential)**:
- Website homepage and key pages
- About/Our Story page
- Existing marketing copy (ads, emails, landing pages)
- Product/service descriptions

**Tier 2 (Valuable)**:
- Blog posts or content articles
- Social media content (last 20-30 posts)
- Email newsletters
- Case studies or testimonials page

**Tier 3 (Supplementary)**:
- Brand guidelines document (if exists)
- Competitor comparison content
- Video scripts or transcripts
- Customer reviews (what customers say about them)

## Extraction Protocol

### Pass 1: First Impressions
Read through all materials without analysing. Note:
- Immediate feeling/impression
- What stands out
- What seems inconsistent
- Overall energy

### Pass 2: Language Analysis
Go through systematically and extract:
- Actual phrases and sentences (copy-paste, don't paraphrase)
- Repeated words (count occurrences)
- Unique terminology or coined phrases
- What they never say (absence is as telling as presence)

### Pass 3: Structure Analysis
- How long are their sentences? (Count words per sentence in 10 samples)
- How long are their paragraphs? (Count sentences per paragraph)
- How do they use punctuation? (Tally: !, ?, —, ..., ;)
- What formatting elements appear? (Bold, lists, headers)

### Pass 4: Audience Analysis
- How do they address the reader? (you/we/they/by name)
- What assumptions do they make about the reader?
- What level of sophistication do they assume?
- What emotions do they address?

# PART 2: VOICE & TONE DIMENSIONS

## Dimension 1: Formality Spectrum
```
1 ─────────────────────────────── 10
Casual                        Formal

Casual markers:
- Contractions (can't, don't, we're)
- Informal words (get, use, make)
- First-person plural (we, us, our)
- Short sentences
- Sentence fragments
- Starting sentences with And/But

Formal markers:
- No contractions
- Technical/precise vocabulary
- Third-person references
- Complete sentence structures
- Citations and references
- Passive voice occasionally
```

## Dimension 2: Energy Level
```
1 ─────────────────────────────── 10
Calm/Measured            High Energy/Urgent

Low energy markers:
- Longer, more deliberate sentences
- Measured, considered language
- Less punctuation variety
- Educational tone
- Minimal urgency language

High energy markers:
- Short, punchy sentences
- Exclamation points
- Action verbs
- Urgency language (now, today, fast)
- Caps for emphasis
- Multiple short paragraphs
```

## Dimension 3: Authority Positioning
```
1 ─────────────────────────────── 10
Peer/Friend              Expert/Authority

Peer markers:
- "We've been there too"
- Vulnerability and challenges shared
- "You're not alone"
- Collaborative language

Authority markers:
- "Our 15 years of experience shows..."
- Data and research citations
- Definitive statements
- "The truth is..."
```

## Dimension 4: Emotional Temperature
```
1 ─────────────────────────────── 10
Neutral/Factual          Emotive/Passionate

Neutral markers:
- Fact-based statements
- Minimal adjectives
- Logical structure
- Data-driven claims

Emotive markers:
- Emotional descriptors
- Story-based content
- Aspirational language
- Empathy statements
- Frustration acknowledged
```

## Dimension 5: Directness
```
1 ─────────────────────────────── 10
Subtle/Suggested         Direct/Explicit

Subtle markers:
- Implied benefits
- Questions instead of statements
- Soft CTAs ("consider", "explore")
- Nuanced language

Direct markers:
- Clear statements of benefit
- Direct CTAs ("buy", "start", "get")
- Bold claims
- "Here's what you get..."
```

# PART 5: BRAND PERSONALITY ARCHETYPES

## The 12 Brand Archetypes
Identify which 1-2 primarily apply:

### 1. The Hero
**Voice**: Bold, confident, competent, inspiring
**Language**: "Conquer", "achieve", "rise", "overcome", "power"
**Tone**: Aspirational, challenging, empowering
**Example brands**: Nike, Under Armour

### 2. The Sage
**Voice**: Wise, analytical, informative, truthful
**Language**: "Research", "insight", "understand", "expertise", "evidence"
**Tone**: Thoughtful, educational, credible
**Example brands**: Google, Harvard

### 3. The Explorer
**Voice**: Adventurous, ambitious, free, pioneering
**Language**: "Discover", "journey", "freedom", "new", "frontier"
**Tone**: Inspiring, restless, authentic
**Example brands**: Patagonia, Jeep

### 4. The Rebel
**Voice**: Disruptive, rule-breaking, daring, provocative
**Language**: "Against", "break", "challenge", "revolution", "refuse"
**Tone**: Confrontational (constructively), authentic, anti-establishment
**Example brands**: Harley-Davidson, Dollar Shave Club

### 5. The Magician
**Voice**: Transformative, visionary, mystical, innovative
**Language**: "Transform", "imagine", "possibilities", "magic", "vision"
**Tone**: Inspiring, mystical, charismatic
**Example brands**: Disney, Apple

### 6. The Jester
**Voice**: Playful, humorous, lighthearted, fun
**Language**: "Play", "fun", "enjoy", "laugh", creative puns
**Tone**: Witty, irreverent, accessible
**Example brands**: Old Spice, Innocent Drinks

### 7. The Lover
**Voice**: Intimate, sensual, empathetic, passionate
**Language**: "Love", "beautiful", "desire", "connection", "indulge"
**Tone**: Warm, inviting, emotionally resonant
**Example brands**: Victoria's Secret, Hallmark

### 8. The Caregiver
**Voice**: Nurturing, supportive, reassuring, protective
**Language**: "Care", "support", "protect", "help", "safe"
**Tone**: Warm, empathetic, service-oriented
**Example brands**: Johnson & Johnson, UNICEF

### 9. The Ruler
**Voice**: Authoritative, refined, commanding, prestigious
**Language**: "Lead", "excellence", "standard", "prestige", "commanding"
**Tone**: Authoritative, exclusive, premium
**Example brands**: Rolex, American Express

### 10. The Creator
**Voice**: Innovative, expressive, imaginative, original
**Language**: "Create", "design", "original", "craft", "build"
**Tone**: Inspired, artistic, visionary
**Example brands**: Adobe, LEGO

### 11. The Innocent
**Voice**: Optimistic, simple, pure, honest, nostalgic
**Language**: "Pure", "simple", "natural", "honest", "wholesome"
**Tone**: Positive, uncomplicated, trustworthy
**Example brands**: Coca-Cola, Dove

### 12. The Everyman
**Voice**: Relatable, down-to-earth, unpretentious, practical
**Language**: "Everyday", "real", "genuine", "practical", "for everyone"

# PART 7: RAPID BRAND MATCHING

## The 5-Minute Brand Check
When you've completed the extraction and need to quickly verify new content matches:

### Read-Aloud Test
Read the content aloud. It should feel like something this brand would say. If it sounds generic, formal, or wrong — it's wrong.

### The Swap Test
Could this exact copy appear on a competitor's site without changing a word? If yes, it's not brand-specific enough.

### The Employee Test
Would a long-term employee of this company cringe or smile reading this? The goal is smile.

### The Customer Test
Would their ideal customer recognise this as being from [brand]? Would it feel familiar?

### The Substitution Test
Take 3 key sentences. Replace the brand name with a competitor's name. If it still sounds right, the copy isn't differentiated enough.

# PART 8: SPECIAL CASES

## When Brand Voice Is Inconsistent
Some brands have evolved over time or have different voices for different channels. In this case:

1. **Identify the most recent content** — this is usually the intended direction
2. **Identify the highest-converting content** — this is what works
3. **Identify the most authentic content** — this is who they really are
4. **Reconcile** — synthesise a voice that honours all three
5. **Document the inconsistencies** — flag them for the client

## When There's Limited Content
If only 1-2 pages of content are available:

1. Use what's there for hard data
2. Note what's missing
3. Make reasonable inferences based on industry + positioning
4. Flag all inferences clearly
5. Recommend gathering more samples before finalising

## When Content Conflicts With Brief
If what the client says about their brand contradicts what their content shows:

1. Document both (stated vs. demonstrated)
2. Note the discrepancy
3. Use demonstrated voice for current content
4. Flag the inconsistency for the client to resolve

# PART 9: INTEGRATION WITH OTHER SKILLS

## Using Brand Style With Ad Creative Pipeline
After extraction:
- Load the vocabulary bank into ad creative briefs
- Apply writing rules to all headlines and body copy
- Use sentence architecture for formatting
- Run the brand check (read-aloud, swap, employee tests) before delivery

## Using Brand Style With Advertorial Architect
- Apply voice dimensions to select the right advertorial format
- Use power words throughout the advertorial
- Ensure proof language matches how the brand typically presents evidence
- CTA should use their specific CTA patterns

## Using Brand Style With Conversion Copy Architect
- Voice profile informs tone throughout the funnel
- Vocabulary bank provides the exact words for bullets and headlines
- Sentence architecture guides rhythm and structure
- Red flags checklist prevents off-brand copy making it to client
